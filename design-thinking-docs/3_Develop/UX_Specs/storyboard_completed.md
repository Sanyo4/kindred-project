# Storyboard: The Routine Preservation Journey -- Completed

**Project:** Kindred
**Step:** 15 (Part G: Visualisation)
**Persona:** Sarah Chen (34, Management Consultant) and her daughter Lily (age 4)
**Journey:** "Routine Preservation" -- the emotional arc from Separation Anxiety to Digital Presence
**Scenario:** Monday evening. Sarah is on a 4-night client trip in Frankfurt (CET, 1hr ahead). Lily is at home in London with partner Alex. Sarah pre-recorded 3 stories on Sunday using Story Banking. Tonight she tries to join live -- but the hotel WiFi drops mid-story. The system handles it. The routine survives.

**Data Sources:** `persona_completed.md`, `service_blueprint_completed.md`, `prioritisation_matrix_completed.md`, `real_win_worth_completed.md`, `design_by_analogy_template.md`

**Emotional Arc:** Guilt/Anxiety --> Relief --> Joy/Connection --> Crisis --> Calm/Safety --> Presence/Pride

## Panel 1: "The Distance" -- The Emotional Starting Point

**Time:** 8:00 PM CET (Frankfurt) / 7:00 PM GMT (London)

### Scene Description

Split scene. **Left:** Sarah sits on the edge of a hotel bed in Frankfurt, still in her work clothes, laptop open with tomorrow's client deck. Her phone shows 8:00 PM. She stares at the Kindred app -- the Trip Planner shows "3 of 4 nights covered" with pre-recorded stories. Tonight is the uncovered night; she planned to read live. **Right:** In London, Lily is in the bathroom with Alex. Bath time is ending. Lily's pyjamas are laid out on her bed next to her iPad, propped on its stand. On the bedside table: the physical copy of The Gruffalo that Mummy usually reads.

### Character Actions

- **Sarah:** Opens Kindred. Checks the Trip Planner -- 3 stories banked (Owl Babies for Tuesday, Room on the Broom for Wednesday, Bear Hunt for Thursday). Monday is marked "Live Session -- overlap window available." She taps "Join Live Tonight" and waits for Lily's session to begin.
- **Lily:** Splashes in the bath. Asks Alex: "Is Mummy reading tonight?" Alex: "Let's see."
- **Alex:** Drains the bath. Thinking about the notification that will arrive in 25 minutes.

### Emotional State

| Character | Emotion | Internal Thought |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Sarah** | Guilt, anxiety, determination | "I've missed bath time for three days. I need to be there for the story at least." |
| **Lily** | Anticipation mixed with uncertainty | "Mummy wasn't here yesterday. Will she be here tonight?" |
| **Alex** | Low-level fatigue from solo parenting since school pickup | "I just need this to be easy tonight." |

### Active MVP Features

- **Story Banking** (Idea 5) -- 3 stories pre-recorded on Sunday, visible in Trip Planner as coverage for Tue-Thu. This is the safety net: even if tonight's live session fails, the rest of the week is covered.

### Validating Evidence

> *"I want to still be part of her routine. Not a guest appearance. Part of it."*
> -- **P1**, Theme 1: "The Routine Must Survive"

**Design Insight:** Sarah's anxiety is not about the technology -- it is about her identity as a present parent. The Trip Planner's "3 of 4 nights covered" counter transforms abstract guilt into a concrete, manageable preparation task. The Spotify analogy: she has built her playlist for the week; tonight is the one live gig.

## Panel 2: "One Tap" -- Session Preparation

**Time:** 7:25 PM GMT (London)

### Scene Description

Lily is in bed in her pyjamas, duvet pulled up, room lamp on. The iPad is propped on its stand at arm's reach. Alex's phone buzzes on the kitchen counter: a push notification with Sarah's photo and tonight's book cover (The Gruffalo) -- *"Story time with Mummy in 5 min -- tap to start."* Alex taps the notification with one thumb while holding a cup of tea in the other hand. The iPad in Lily's room lights up with a warm, soft-blue splash screen: Sarah's face in a circle, The Gruffalo cover below, and a large illustrated button reading "Open Book" with an audio label that says the words aloud. Alex walks back to the kitchen. Total involvement: under 10 seconds.

### Character Actions

- **Alex:** Sees notification. Taps it. Doesn't need to enter the bedroom. The deep-link pre-authenticated the session -- no login, no navigation, no book selection required. Returns to cleaning up the kitchen.
- **Lily:** Sees the splash screen. Hears a gentle chime and the words "Open Book." Sees Mummy's face. Taps the big button. The screen transitions to The Gruffalo's first page with a warm, dimmed colour palette -- soft blues and muted golds. No bright whites.
- **Sarah:** Receives *"Lily is ready!"* on her phone. Joins the live session from the hotel room.

### Emotional State

| Character | Emotion | Internal Thought |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Alex** | Relief -- it took one tap and zero troubleshooting | "That's it. Done. She's sorted." |
| **Lily** | Excitement -- Mummy's face appeared on the screen | "Mummy!" |
| **Sarah** | Anticipation -- the session is starting | "She's there. We're doing this." |

### Active MVP Features

- **One-Tap Bedtime Launch** (Idea 9) -- Push notification with deep-link to pre-selected story. No login, no navigation. The adoption gatekeeper: all four relevant interview participants independently cited one-tap as the threshold for caregiver compliance.
- **Progressive Dimming Interface** (Idea 18) -- The UI opens in a warm bedtime palette (soft blues, muted golds, no bright whites). Screen brightness is already reduced from the default. This is the app's first visual signal that it belongs in a bedroom at 7:30 PM, not a living room at 3 PM.

### Validating Evidence

> *"One button. That's it. One button or she can't do it."*
> -- **P3**, Theme 3: "The Third Hand Problem"

**Design Insight:** The Uber analogy -- your destination is predicted, your payment is saved, the car comes to you. Alex's cognitive load is identical to hailing a ride: one tap and the experience begins. This is why Alex uses the app and the grandmother could too. The threshold is not "tech literacy" -- it is "number of taps at 7:25 PM after solo parenting since 3:30."

## Panel 3: "Reading Together" -- The Live Shared Reading

**Time:** 7:28 PM GMT (London) / 8:28 PM CET (Frankfurt)

### Scene Description

Lily's iPad shows The Gruffalo open to a double-page spread: the mouse walking through the deep dark wood. In the top-right corner, a small picture-in-picture circle shows Mummy's face, warmly lit by the hotel desk lamp, reading aloud. Sarah's voice fills the bedroom: *"A mouse took a stroll through the deep dark wood..."* Lily taps the page to turn it -- the page turns simultaneously on Sarah's screen in Frankfurt. Both see the same page at the same moment. On the new page, the Sleepy Owl character -- perched in the corner of the illustration, separate from the story art -- produces a thought bubble: *"Can you find something brown?"* The owl's voice says the words aloud in a gentle, curious tone. Lily scans the page, taps the mouse. The owl reacts with a small nod: *"The mouse! That's right!"*

### Character Actions

- **Sarah:** Reads aloud from the hotel room. Sees Lily's face in her own PIP corner -- Lily is focused on the page, not wandering. Sarah sees the prompt appear on her own screen and watches Lily's response in real time. She is three pages in. Lily has not looked away once.
- **Lily:** Listens to Mummy's voice. Taps the page to advance. When the owl asks "Can you find something brown?", she looks carefully at the illustration, then taps the mouse. The owl confirms. She grins. She taps a fox in the background -- the fox says *"Fox!"* (audio label). She is engaged, active, playing -- not passively watching a talking head.
- **The Sleepy Owl:** Perched in the corner of the page. Delivers PEER/CROWD prompts as thought bubbles with voice. This is a Wh-question (CROWD framework) -- appropriate for a first reading. On repeated readings, the owl will escalate to Recall and Distancing prompts: *"Last time the mouse was brave -- do you think he's still brave?"*

### Emotional State

| Character | Emotion | Internal Thought |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Sarah** | Joy, connection, felt-presence | "She's focused. She's playing. This is what bedtime should feel like -- even from here." |
| **Lily** | Engaged, playful, secure | She is not thinking about the screen or the distance. She is thinking about the mouse and the fox and where the brown thing is. The parent's voice + the shared book + the owl's questions have collapsed the distance. |

### Active MVP Features

- **Synchronised Page-Turn with Shared Touch** (Idea 1) -- Both devices show the same page. Lily's tap advances both screens simultaneously via WebSocket `page_sync` events (<100ms latency). This is the shared object of attention that video calls lack -- the Google Docs analogy: both cursors on the same document.
- **Live Dialogic Reading Prompts -- PEER/CROWD** (Idea 3) -- The owl delivers page-specific prompts calibrated to reading number. First reading: Completion and Wh-questions. Third reading of the same book: Recall and Distancing. This is the mechanism Raffle et al. (2010) found extended engagement from 2-3 minutes to 15-20 minutes.
- **Sleepy Owl Guide Character** (Idea 11) -- The character delivery vehicle for dialogic prompts. Replaces visual-only cues (Caribu's approach -- the least effective prompt type per Hiniker et al.) with audio instruction + character demonstration (the second and third most effective types). The owl is also progressively getting sleepier as the story progresses -- modelling the wind-down Lily should follow.

### Validating Evidence

> *"The book gives him something to focus on. That's what's missing from a video call -- there's no shared object between us."*
> -- **P2**, Theme 2: "Nothing to Do Together"

**Design Insight:** The distinction is structural: Lily is not having a conversation (a capacity preschoolers aged 2-4 cannot sustain -- Yarosh & Abowd, 2011). She is playing. The owl's prompts transform passive page-turning into "a creative shared activity that suggests a new kind of collaborative storytelling" (Raffle et al., 2010). This is why she is three pages in and has not looked away -- compare to P1's 2-minute ceiling on FaceTime.

## Panel 4: "The Drop" -- Connection Breaks Mid-Story

**Time:** 7:35 PM GMT (London) / 8:35 PM CET (Frankfurt)

### Scene Description

Page 8 of The Gruffalo. Sarah is mid-sentence: *"He has terrible tusks, and terrible cla--"* The hotel WiFi drops. **On Sarah's screen:** The live feed freezes, then a calm message appears: *"Connection interrupted -- Lily's story is continuing with your recording."* Below it: a "Reconnect?" button and a timestamp showing which page Lily is on. **On Lily's screen:** Nothing alarming happens. The owl, who has been perched in the corner, slides gently to the centre of the page. It raises one wing to its beak in a "shh" gesture. A soft voice says: *"Shh... let's keep reading while Mummy gets comfortable!"* A brief 3-second animation -- the owl settles back to its perch -- bridges the silence. Then: Sarah's voice resumes. But it is now the pre-recorded version from Sunday's Story Banking session, crossfaded seamlessly from the live audio. The recording picks up from page 8 -- the exact page where the connection dropped. Sarah's recorded PIP face replaces the live feed with a 0.5-second fade. The story continues without a single moment of silence or error.

### Character Actions

- **Sarah:** Sees the interruption message. Her first instinct is panic -- but the message says the story is continuing. She exhales. She tries to reconnect; the WiFi is unstable. She watches the status: "Lily is on page 9... page 10..." The system is handling it. She does not need to call Alex. She does not need to do anything.
- **Lily:** Sees the owl do a "shh" animation. Thinks it is part of the story -- owls say "shh." Mummy's voice continues reading. The page turns. She taps the next prompt. She does not know the connection dropped. She does not know the voice is now a recording. She does not care. The mouse is about to meet the Gruffalo and that is all that matters.
- **The Sleepy Owl:** Performs the Character-Mediated Bridge. This is the single most important 3 seconds in the entire product: the moment where a technical infrastructure event (WebSocket timeout after 3 missed pings at 5-second intervals) is transformed into a story-world event (the owl says "shh"). The child's experience is unbroken. The Spotify crossfade analogy: the music never stops.

### Emotional State

| Character | Emotion | Internal Thought |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Sarah** | Momentary worry --> relief --> gratitude | "It dropped. But she's still reading. She doesn't know. Oh thank God she doesn't know." |
| **Lily** | Uninterrupted engagement | She has no internal thought about the connection because she has no awareness it changed. The owl said "shh" and Mummy kept reading. That is all. |

### Active MVP Features

- **Sync-to-Async Seamless Handoff** (Idea 15) -- The core architectural differentiator. WebSocket drop detected after 15 seconds (3 missed pings at 5s intervals). System locates the pre-cached async recording of The Gruffalo (recorded Sunday via Story Banking). Seeks to `current_page_index` (page 8). Crossfades live audio to recorded audio. Transitions session mode from SYNC to ASYNC_FALLBACK. Zero-competition space: FaceTime shows "Call Failed." Caribu terminates the session. Kindred keeps reading.
- **Character-Mediated Handoff Bridge** (SCAMPER 1.2) -- The owl's 3-second "shh" animation buffers the audio transition. Transforms "connection dropped" into "owl says shh." Prevents the worst emotional outcome in the entire problem space: a child perceiving a technical disconnection as parental abandonment.
- **Audio-Only Recording Over Animated Pages** (SCAMPER 1.1) -- The pre-recorded narration that makes the handoff possible. Sarah recorded this on Sunday, seeing the book pages on her screen (not a camera feed of herself), which removed the self-consciousness barrier P5 identified. Lily hears Mummy's voice over the illustrated pages -- identical experience to the live session.

### Validating Evidence

> *"I had to say Daddy has to go NOW and hang up. The twin screamed for twenty minutes afterwards."*
> -- **P4**, Theme 4: "When Connection Breaks"

**Design Insight:** This panel depicts the moment Kindred was designed to prevent. P4's incident is the most emotionally damaging event in the entire dataset. P3 described the inverse: *"The screen freezes on his face and I can hear him saying Mummy but I can't respond. It's worse than not calling."* The 5 Whys analysis (Analyses 1, 3, and 5) converged on root causes B2 (real-time dependency) and B5 (no sync+async combination) as the deepest structural failures. The Character-Mediated Bridge + Sync-to-Async Handoff are the direct engineering response. A failed connection is no longer psychologically costlier than no connection -- it is invisible.

## Panel 5: "Goodnight, Owl" -- The Transition to Sleep

**Time:** 7:48 PM GMT (London)

### Scene Description

The Gruffalo is finished. The last page fades gently. Sarah's recorded voice says: *"And the mouse found a nut and the nut was good. Goodnight, Lily. Sleep tight, my love."* (The personalised goodnight message Sarah recorded at the end of Sunday's session.) The screen begins to dim -- progressively, like a sunset. The warm blues deepen. The text and illustrations fade. The Sleepy Owl, who has been getting drowsier throughout the story (eyes half-closed during the last three pages, occasional yawns between prompts), now curls up on a moonlit branch in the centre of the dimmed screen. It tucks its head under its wing. A single gentle breath animation -- the wing rises and falls slowly. The screen is now very dim -- a soft amber glow, like a nightlight. The owl is asleep. Lily's room is quiet. Her eyes are heavy. The iPad glows faintly on its stand beside her bed.

### Character Actions

- **Lily:** Watches the owl fall asleep. Her own eyes mirror the owl's -- getting heavy, blinking slower. The story finished. Mummy's voice said goodnight. The owl is sleeping. Everything is as it should be. She pulls the duvet up. She does not ask for extra stories. She does not ask for the nightlight -- the sleeping owl is the nightlight.
- **The Sleepy Owl:** Completes its wind-down arc. Across the story's 14 pages, the owl transitioned through four states: awake (curious, asking questions) --> cosy (leaning on the branch, softer voice) --> sleepy (eyes half-closed, yawning between prompts) --> asleep (curled up, gentle breathing). This progression modelled the emotional trajectory Lily should follow -- the Headspace analogy: the content doesn't end when the meditation ends; it transitions you into sleep. The owl, now asleep on screen, functions as a digital transitional comfort object (Winnicott, 1953) -- not a UI element, but a presence.

### Emotional State

| Character | Emotion | Internal Thought |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Lily** | Calm, safe, sleepy | The routine completed. Mummy read the story. The owl is asleep. The world is predictable and warm. No anxiety, no stalling, no requests for water or extra stories. |
| **Sarah** (not visible in this panel, but present in the emotional arc) | Has not yet received the completion notification -- she is still in the hotel, hoping the recording finished | "Did she hear the whole thing? Is she okay?" |

### Active MVP Features

- **Progressive Dimming Interface** (Idea 18) -- Screen brightness and colour warmth modulate across the final pages of the story, reaching near-minimum by the goodnight screen. No competitor modulates their UI for the bedtime context. This is the "nightlight, not a television" design principle from the analogy template. The CSS brightness/warmth transitions are trivial to implement but disproportionately impact the product's perceived appropriateness for the bedtime moment.
- **Owl as Transitional Comfort Object** (SCAMPER 2.1) -- The sleeping owl on the dimmed screen fills the emotionally vulnerable gap between "story over" and "falling asleep" -- a gap no competitor acknowledges exists. FaceTime: "Call Ended." Caribu: home screen. Kindred: a sleeping companion. The ambient glow remains for a configurable duration (default: 20 minutes) before the screen turns off entirely.

### Validating Evidence

> *"He asks for extra stories. He wants to sleep with the light on which he hasn't needed for over a year."*
> -- **P5**, Theme 4: "When Connection Breaks"

**Design Insight:** P5's son (age 7) regresses to comfort-seeking behaviours after just 3-5 days of parental absence: outgrown books, nightlight dependency, bedtime stalling. The Owl Comfort Object directly addresses the nightlight need -- a warm digital presence that says "someone is still here." The Tamagotchi analogy inverts: instead of the child caring for the virtual pet, the virtual pet cares for the child. The owl fell asleep first, giving Lily permission to follow.

## Panel 6: "Constant Presence" -- Resolution

**Time:** 8:05 PM CET (Frankfurt) / 7:05 PM next-day emotional state

### Scene Description

**Frankfurt:** Sarah's phone buzzes. A Kindred notification: *"Lily's story is complete! The Gruffalo -- all 14 pages. 4 prompts answered. Session: 20 minutes."* Below: the Routine Dose Tracker -- a constellation of sleeping stars, each representing one consecutive night of completed bedtime stories. Four stars glow softly. *"4 consecutive nights."* Sarah scrolls down: a session summary showing that the mode transitioned from SYNC to ASYNC_FALLBACK at page 8, but the story completed in full. Lily engaged with all four owl prompts. The session counts as complete. The streak is intact. **London (morning after):** Lily wakes up. The iPad screen is dark -- the owl's nightlight glow faded after 20 minutes. She does not mention the WiFi dropping because she does not know it happened. At breakfast, she tells Alex: *"Mummy read The Gruffalo. The owl went to sleep."* The routine survived.

### Character Actions

- **Sarah:** Reads the completion notification. Sees the 4-night streak. Exhales. The guilt that opened Panel 1 has not disappeared, but it has been restructured: she is not "absent from bedtime" -- she is "present through a different channel." She taps the Trip Planner: Tuesday's Owl Babies is already queued. She closes the laptop. She can sleep.
- **Lily:** (Next morning.) Talks about the story naturally, as though Mummy were in the room. The story is real; the distance is not. The routine is intact. No behavioural regression. No stalling. No requests for the nightlight -- the owl was the nightlight.
- **Alex:** (Next morning.) Did not re-enter the bedroom after the one-tap notification. Found Lily asleep when checking at 8:15 PM. Did not need to troubleshoot, re-initiate, or intervene.

### Emotional State

| Character | Emotion | Internal Thought |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Sarah** | Guilt reduced --> pride, felt-presence confirmed | "Four nights. I didn't miss one. Even when the WiFi dropped, she still heard me. I'm still part of it." |
| **Lily** | Secure, routine-anchored | Mummy reads a story every night. Sometimes Mummy is on the screen, sometimes her voice comes from the owl. It doesn't matter. The story happens. The routine is real. |
| **Alex** | Relieved, validated in tool adoption | "It worked. I tapped one button. She's asleep. I'll use it again tomorrow." |

### Active MVP Features

- **Story Banking** (Idea 5) -- The pre-recorded Gruffalo narration (recorded Sunday) is what made the sync-to-async handoff possible. Without it, the story would have been lost at page 8. Story Banking is not just an async feature -- it is the safety net that guarantees the routine survives any infrastructure failure. P4: *"The core idea of banking content when I'm free and deploying it when I'm not -- that makes complete sense."*
- **Routine Dose Tracker** (Service Blueprint Section 6) -- The sleeping-star constellation visualises Mindell et al.'s (2015) dose-dependent finding: each consecutive night with a completed bedtime story strengthens the protective association with sleep outcomes. 4 stars = 4 nights = routine intact. This is the Snapchat Streak analogy -- a visible, motivating feedback loop that rewards continuity. The tracker does not distinguish between sync, async, or fallback sessions. A completed story is a completed story. The routine survived.

### Validating Evidence

> *"He doesn't care that it's not live. He just wants to hear my voice doing the thing."*
> -- **P2**, Theme 1: "The Routine Must Survive"

**Design Insight:** This quote is the emotional thesis of the entire product. P2's son (age 3) watches the same pre-recorded video 3-4 times with sustained engagement but disengages from live calls in 5 seconds. For preschoolers, presence is carried by voice + shared activity, not by the live video feed itself. The storyboard's resolution proves it: Lily did not notice that the voice switched from live to recorded. She did not care. She heard Mummy. She heard the story. She fell asleep. That is "constant presence" (Yarosh & Abowd, 2011). Not constant video. Constant voice. Constant ritual. Constant presence.
