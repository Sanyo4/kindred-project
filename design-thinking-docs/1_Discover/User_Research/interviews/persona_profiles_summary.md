## Persona Profiles

### P1 (34) -- "The Frequent Flyer"

**Role:** Management Consultant at a Big Four firm, London

**Family:** Partner works from home as a graphic designer. Daughter, age 4.

**Travel Pattern:** Away Monday to Thursday most weeks. Flies to Frankfurt, Paris, Amsterdam regularly. Occasional US trips (New York, Chicago) with 5-8 hour time difference.

**Tech Profile:** High comfort. Uses iPad, iPhone, MacBook daily. Daughter has her own iPad. Partner is also tech-literate.

**Current Approach:** FaceTime calls at bedtime. Has tried reading books on camera -- finds it awkward. Sometimes sends WhatsApp voice notes of herself reading a story for her daughter to listen to later.

**Emotional Profile:** Articulate about her frustrations. Has "tried everything" and developed workarounds. Feels guilty but channels it into action. Pragmatic tone with occasional emotional moments.

**What She Tests:** The core Problem Tree assumptions -- no shared context (B1), child distraction during calls (A1), and the facilitation burden on the partner (C3). She is the "baseline confirming" persona.

### P2 (41) -- "The Long-Haul Traveler"

**Role:** Regional Sales Director at a tech company, Cardiff

**Family:** Spouse is a full-time nurse, works rotating shifts. Son, age 3. Older daughter, age 6. P2's mother (grandmother) helps with childcare when spouse works nights.

**Travel Pattern:** 1-2 week trips quarterly to US West Coast, Singapore, Japan. When he's in Singapore, bedtime in Cardiff is his mid-morning meeting time.

**Tech Profile:** Moderate. Uses work laptop and phone competently but is not an early adopter. The family iPad is old and slow. Spouse uses an Android phone.

**Current Approach:** WhatsApp video calls when time zones allow. Has largely accepted that live bedtime calls are impossible during Asia trips. Sends "good morning" videos that the kids watch at breakfast instead.

**Emotional Profile:** Measured, slightly resigned. Has rationalised the separation ("the kids are fine, spouse handles it"). Deeper probing reveals sadness about missing developmental milestones. More emotional about son (3) who barely engages on calls than older daughter (6) who can hold a conversation.

**What He Tests:** Time zone extremes (C2), the absence of sync+async combined (B5). Also reveals complexity when the caregiver switches between spouse and grandparent, and when two children at different developmental stages need different approaches.

### P3 (29) -- "The Reluctant Tech User"

**Role:** Oil Rig Safety Inspector, Aberdeen. Works a FIFO (fly-in, fly-out) rotation: 2 weeks on the rig, 2 weeks home.

**Family:** Single mother. Son, age 5. Her mother (grandmother) is the primary caregiver during the 2 weeks away. Grandmother speaks English but is not comfortable with technology.

**Travel Pattern:** 14 consecutive days offshore with very limited and unreliable internet (satellite connection on the rig). Then 14 days at home with full routine.

**Tech Profile:** Low. Uses a basic Android phone. Finds most apps overwhelming. Bought a cheap tablet for son but it's slow. Grandmother uses an even older phone and needs help with anything beyond phone calls.

**Current Approach:** Regular phone calls when the satellite signal allows (often drops). Has tried video calls but the lag and freezing make them worse than voice-only. Cannot reliably video call at bedtime.

**Emotional Profile:** The most emotionally raw persona. Single-parent guilt is compounded by the physical impossibility of connecting. Uses short, direct sentences. Gets visibly upset when describing son asking "When is Mummy coming home?" Fiercely protective of whatever connection she can maintain.

**What She Tests:** Challenges the assumption that time zones are the primary barrier (C2) -- for her, it's connectivity and tech literacy. Confirms caregiver burden at the extreme end (C3) where the caregiver cannot troubleshoot technology. Surfaces the "grandparent facilitator" gap not fully captured in the Problem Tree.

### P4 (37) -- "The On-Call Dad"

**Role:** A&E (Emergency Department) Doctor, Bristol. Works rotating shifts including frequent overnight and weekend shifts.

**Family:** Spouse is a part-time primary school teacher, primary bedtime caregiver. Twin daughters, age 5.

**Travel Pattern:** Not traditional travel. Sleeps at the hospital 2-3 nights per week. Sometimes home but arrives after bedtime. Occasionally has a gap during his shift but can't guarantee it. Lives 20 minutes from the hospital.

**Tech Profile:** High. Tech-savvy, uses multiple devices comfortably. Has an iPad Pro at home and his phone at work.

**Current Approach:** FaceTime calls when he can, but rarely at bedtime specifically because (a) he's often in the middle of a shift, (b) calling and then having to suddenly hang up when a patient arrives makes the twins upset, and (c) spouse finds the unpredictability more disruptive than helpful.

**Emotional Profile:** Initially presents as stoic and practical ("It is what it is, shift work"). But contradicts himself -- later describes how it "kills him" when the twins ask why Daddy isn't there. Analytical about the problem. Quick to identify what would and wouldn't work for his schedule.

**What He Tests:** Challenges the fundamental framing of "travel separation" -- P4 is in the same city. His constraint is schedule, not geography or time zones. Confirms asymmetric motivations (C4): calling and then abruptly leaving is worse than not calling at all. Reveals that pre-recorded stories during shift breaks could solve his specific problem.

### P5 (44) -- "The Conference Traveler"

**Role:** University Lecturer in Education Studies, Manchester. Attends 3-4 academic conferences per year, each lasting 3-5 days.

**Family:** Partner is a stay-at-home dad. Son, age 7 (at the upper boundary of the target age range).

**Travel Pattern:** Occasional: ~15-20 nights per year, clustered around conference season (usually September-November and March-April). Conferences in Europe and occasionally the US.

**Tech Profile:** Moderate-high. Comfortable with mainstream apps (Zoom from teaching). Less likely to download niche or new apps unless she sees clear value.

**Current Approach:** Zoom calls at bedtime when time zones allow. Has tried reading books on camera and it "sort of works" because son is old enough to follow along. Sometimes just chats with him about his day instead.

**Emotional Profile:** Reflective and analytical (academic mindset). Doesn't describe intense guilt but acknowledges that even 3-4 missed bedtimes in a row feel significant. Frames the problem intellectually ("routine consistency matters, I know the research"). More likely to ask questions about the app's evidence base.

**What She Tests:** The "mild case" -- is Kindred overkill for someone who travels infrequently? Her answer reveals that even brief disruptions feel significant, validating Mindell's dose-dependent finding (C5). Also tests the age-7 boundary: son can read basic text and hold conversations, so some developmental assumptions from Hiniker shift. Provides the counterpoint perspective on whether the solution scales across ages.
