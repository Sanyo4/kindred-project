# Solution Tree Analysis

This solution tree maps the desired future state for the remote bedtime storytelling app. Each negative situation from the Problem Tree is inverted into a positive goal, each root cause becomes a concrete solution strategy, and each consequence becomes a measurable objective.

## 1. Vision / Desirable Outcome (The Trunk)

**Separated families sustain a consistent, developmentally appropriate bedtime routine through an activity-based digital tool that fosters meaningful parent–child connection and preserves sleep-related rituals during periods of work-related absence.**

This vision directly inverts the core problem. Where the problem identified the *lack* of such a tool — and the resulting degradation of connection and routine — the solution provides the tool and, through it, restores both. "Activity-based" is deliberate: Raffle et al. (2010) demonstrated that shared activity (not conversation) is the interaction mode young children can sustain, and Yarosh & Abowd (2011) showed that routine-embedded communication achieves "constant presence" more effectively than standalone calls.

## 2. Actions, Strategies & Solutions (The Roots)

### A. Addressing Developmental Needs (Child-Centric Design)

Each strategy below directly addresses a root cause from **Section 2A** of the Problem Tree (Developmental / Cognitive Factors).

- **Strategy A1 — Replace conversation with structured shared activity (addresses: "Preschoolers lack conversational skills").** Anchor every remote session around a shared digital storybook rather than free-form video chat. Raffle et al. (2010) found that "even when co-located adults interact with young children they do not have a conversation, but rather they play together." The app's primary interaction loop is therefore joint page-turning, character interaction, and dialogic prompts — not talking heads. Both parent and child see the same story page simultaneously, giving them a shared object of attention and joint context that standard video calls lack.

- **Strategy A2 — Implement character-guided dialogic reading prompts using the PEER/CROWD framework (addresses: "Young children need shared activity to sustain engagement").** An animated in-app character delivers page-specific CROWD-type prompts (Completion, Recall, Open-ended, Wh-questions, Distancing) via touchable "thought bubble" trigger points, directly replicating the Elmo mechanism from Raffle et al. (2010). Prompts are sequenced developmentally: sessions begin with simpler types (Completion, Wh-questions) and progress to harder types (Recall, Distancing) across repeated readings. Emotion-focused prompts (e.g., "How do you think the bear feels?") are front-loaded, as Raffle et al. found these generated the strongest engagement. The remote parent retains control over prompt timing, enabling responsive, well-timed interventions rather than fixed automation.

- **Strategy A3 — Use multimodal prompts: concrete audio instruction paired with animated hand demonstration (addresses: "Comprehension, not motor skill, is the primary barrier" and "Visual-only prompts are ineffective").** Following Hiniker et al.'s (2015) hierarchy — Model > Audio ≈ Hand >> Visual — every interactive element combines at least two prompt modalities. Touchscreen gestures are accompanied by a concrete, imperative audio instruction (e.g., "Tap the star two times") and an animated cartoon hand demonstrating the action with a visible contact dot. Visual state changes (pulsing, glowing) are used only as attention-getters, never as the sole communicator of what action is required. This directly addresses Hiniker et al.'s finding that visual-only prompts appeared in 41% of children's apps yet were the least effective prompt type.

- **Strategy A4 — Implement escalating scaffolding with the remote parent as the "model prompt" gold standard (addresses: "Children under 3 cannot respond to any in-app prompt without an adult co-user").** The app implements a three-tier scaffolding cascade: (1) audio + visual cue as the default prompt, (2) character hand demonstration if no response within a configurable timeout, and (3) a prompt to the remote parent to physically model the gesture via the live video feed. Hiniker et al. (2015) established adult modeling as the only effective instructional method for children under 3 and the most effective method across all ages. By positioning the parent's live video as the final escalation tier, the app leverages the strongest available prompt while simultaneously reinforcing the parent–child interaction.

- **Strategy A5 — Design all interactions for cognitive accessibility using Hiniker's age-threshold findings (addresses: "Audio and hand prompts are strongly age-dependent, with gains between ages 3 and 3.5").** The app offers an age-adaptive mode. For children aged 3.5 and above, in-app audio and character demonstration prompts operate independently. For children under 3.5, the app defaults to a "guided mode" that foregrounds the parent's video feed as the primary instructional channel, with in-app prompts serving as secondary reinforcement. This maps directly to Hiniker et al.'s (2015) data showing sharp performance gains between ages 3 and 3.5 for audio and hand prompts.

### B. Improving Technology & Tools (App Features)

Each strategy below directly addresses a root cause from **Section 2B** of the Problem Tree (Technological Factors).

- **Strategy B1 — Implement real-time WebSocket synchronisation for continuous shared application state (addresses: "Existing tools offer no mechanism for synchronising a shared reading experience").** A WebSocket event architecture emits page-turn events, touch interactions, and reading position in real time, targeting sub-100ms latency. Both devices display the same page, the same highlighted text, and the same interactive elements simultaneously. This digitally replicates the sensor-enhanced book frame and remote page icon from Raffle et al. (2010) — the page-sync mechanism that enabled shared context — but eliminates the specialised hardware dependency entirely. The parent's device shows a synchronised view of the child's current page plus a small video feed of the child; the child's device shows the story with overlaid character prompts and a small video feed of the parent.

- **Strategy B2 — Provide an asynchronous fallback mode for time-zone mismatches and unstable connections (addresses: "No existing tool combines synchronous and asynchronous modes for family routines").** When schedules or time zones prevent live reading, the traveling parent can pre-record a narrated story session — reading aloud, triggering dialogic prompts, and leaving personalised audio messages at specific pages. The child plays this recording at their own bedtime, with the story pages advancing in sync with the parent's recorded narration. This directly implements Yarosh & Abowd's (2011) "scheduled synchronous + spontaneous asynchronous" model within a single activity. If a live WebSocket connection drops mid-session, the app automatically transitions to the most recent async recording for that story, ensuring the bedtime routine is never interrupted by technical failure.

- **Strategy B3 — Replace text-based and adult-centric UI patterns with character demonstrations and audio-first interaction (addresses: "Current app interfaces ignore preschooler cognitive and motor limitations" and "Video communication tools were designed for adult conversation patterns").** The child-facing interface contains no text-based prompts and no abstract iconography. Navigation uses large, illustrated tap targets with audio labels that play on hover/long-press. Character demonstrations (the most effective non-adult prompt at 3% prevalence in existing apps per Hiniker et al., 2015) guide every new interaction. The parent-facing interface is a separate, adult-optimised view with text controls, session settings, and prompt management — separating the two user experiences entirely rather than forcing a single UI to serve both.

- **Strategy B4 — Provide a shared digital storybook as the persistent object of joint attention (addresses: "Standard video chat provides no shared object of attention or joint context").** The digital storybook is the central shared artefact. Both parent and child see the same illustrated page, can point to elements (with synchronised touch indicators), and interact with the same character prompts. This directly creates the "shared context and joint attention" that Raffle et al. (2010) identified as missing from standard video calls. The parent's pointer/touch is visible on the child's screen as a distinct cursor (e.g., a hand icon), enabling deictic reference ("Look at this!") — a behaviour Raffle et al. observed emerging naturally during Story Play sessions.

- **Strategy B5 — Minimise setup complexity to reduce the collocated adult's technical burden (addresses: "Video communication tools required fixed locations, significant setup time").** Session initiation requires a single tap on a scheduled story notification. The at-home caregiver receives a push notification at the scheduled bedtime ("Story time with Dad in 5 minutes — tap to set up"), opens the app, and hands the device to the child. The remote parent joins from their device. No account configuration, no call setup, no device pairing is required per-session. This directly addresses Yarosh & Abowd's (2011) finding that the collocated adult bears a heavy facilitation burden, and eliminates the hardware complexity of Raffle et al.'s sensor-enhanced frame.

### C. Supporting the Context (Routine & Environment)

Each strategy below directly addresses a root cause from **Section 2C** of the Problem Tree (Contextual / Environmental Factors).

- **Strategy C1 — Embed the app into the existing bedtime ritual through scheduled, recurring sessions (addresses: "Work-related separation is recurring, predictable, and pervasive").** The app supports a recurring schedule (e.g., "Every weeknight at 7:30 PM child's local time") that mirrors the family's existing bedtime routine. Because work separation is predictable and recurring (Yarosh & Abowd, 2011), the app can pre-schedule an entire trip's worth of reading sessions at setup. Notifications remind both parents and the child. Mindell et al. (2015) established that bedtime routine consistency has a dose-dependent positive effect on sleep outcomes — the app's scheduling mechanism directly maximises the "dose" by ensuring no night is skipped.

- **Strategy C2 — Support time-zone adaptation with the async recording mode and a "bedtime clock" feature (addresses: "Time zone differences make synchronous bedtime sessions impossible on many nights").** When the remote parent is in a time zone where the child's bedtime falls during their working hours, the app prompts the parent to pre-record that night's story during a convenient window. A "bedtime clock" display shows both the parent's and child's local times, highlighting overlap windows for live sessions and flagging nights that require async mode. This ensures the routine happens every night regardless of geography, directly addressing Yarosh & Abowd's (2011) finding that families lacked effective fallback mechanisms when schedules did not align.

- **Strategy C3 — Reduce the collocated adult's facilitation burden through one-tap setup and child-safe autonomous interaction (addresses: "The collocated adult bears a heavy facilitation burden").** Beyond the one-tap session initiation (Strategy B5), the app's child-facing interface is designed for supervised autonomy: once the at-home caregiver opens the app and hands the device to the child, the character guide and the remote parent manage the interaction. The at-home adult does not need to mediate, troubleshoot, or coax participation. A "caregiver dashboard" provides a passive summary of session activity (duration, pages read, prompts completed) without requiring real-time involvement. This directly lightens the burden identified by Yarosh & Abowd (2011), where the collocated adult had to "set up technology, coax the child into participating, and manage the interaction."

- **Strategy C4 — Make engagement intrinsically appealing to the child rather than parent-imposed (addresses: "Parent and child motivations for contact are asymmetric").** The character guide, interactive story elements, and touch-triggered prompts are designed to make the reading session feel like play rather than a mandated phone call. The child has agency — choosing which thought bubbles to tap, which prompts to answer, and which story to read next. Yarosh & Abowd (2011) found that children perceived forced contact as "boring" or "intrusive"; by making the activity child-directed and playful, the app aligns the child's motivation (wanting to play) with the parent's motivation (wanting to connect). Progress rewards (e.g., unlocking new stories, earning character stickers) provide additional intrinsic motivation without high stimulation.

- **Strategy C5 — Prioritise calm, low-stimulation design to protect the bedtime context (addresses: "Bedtime is a uniquely high-stakes routine window").** The app uses a muted colour palette (warm blues, soft purples, low contrast), gentle animations, and progressively decreasing brightness as the story advances. Audio prompts use a calm, measured tone. No flashing elements, no game-like reward sounds, no high-energy interactions. This is a deliberate inversion of the Problem Tree's identification that "current video tools have high stimulation and can disrupt sleeping." Mindell et al. (2015) demonstrated that disrupting the bedtime routine has measurable sleep consequences; the app's visual and audio design actively supports the wind-down process rather than counteracting it.

## 3. Goals & Objectives (The Branches)

### A. Immediate Outcomes (User Experience)

Each outcome below is the direct inverse of an immediate consequence from **Section 3A** of the Problem Tree.

- **Outcome A1 — Children sustain engaged participation for materially longer than unstructured video calls (inverts: "Children become bored, distracted, or refuse to engage").** Later StoryVisit reporting is often cited as showing substantially longer interactions when shared reading and character-guided prompts are used. The target benchmark here is a median session duration of 15+ minutes, measured via in-app session logging.

- **Outcome A2 — Children successfully complete 80%+ of in-app interactions without adult intervention (inverts: "Children experience frustration when they cannot understand app interactions").** Hiniker et al. (2015) found that when children understood a gesture, they executed it successfully 87% of the time. By ensuring comprehension through multimodal prompts (Strategy A3), the app targets an 80% independent interaction completion rate for children aged 3.5+, measured by logging prompt-response pairs and escalation events.

- **Outcome A3 — The at-home caregiver's active involvement is limited to session initiation (inverts: "The at-home caregiver experiences increased stress from the facilitation burden").** Measured by tracking caregiver interaction events during sessions. The target is that after the initial one-tap setup, the collocated adult performs zero required interactions during the reading session. Post-session caregiver surveys (SUS or similar) should yield satisfaction scores above 70 for ease of use.

- **Outcome A4 — The remote parent reports feeling actively involved in the child's bedtime routine (inverts: "Traveling parents experience guilt and a sense of exclusion").** Measured via post-session Likert-scale items on perceived involvement, closeness, and satisfaction. Yarosh & Abowd (2011) identified that parents sought "constant presence" — the target is a mean score of 4+ out of 5 on a "felt presence" scale after synchronous sessions, and 3+ after asynchronous sessions.

- **Outcome A5 — Sessions complete without technical disruption on 90%+ of attempts (inverts: "Remote reading sessions collapse rapidly without scaffolding").** The combination of WebSocket sync (Strategy B1) with automatic async fallback (Strategy B2) ensures that no session is lost to connectivity failure. Measured by logging session completion rates and fallback activation frequency.

### B. Long-term Impacts

Each impact below is the direct inverse of a long-term consequence from **Section 3B** of the Problem Tree.

- **Impact B1 — Maintained bedtime routine consistency during separation periods, supporting positive sleep outcomes (inverts: "Degraded bedtime routine consistency leads to poorer sleep outcomes").** Mindell et al. (2015) established the dose-dependent relationship between routine consistency and sleep quality. The app's scheduling system (Strategy C1) and async fallback (Strategy B2) aim to maintain routine frequency at 5+ nights per week during separation, preserving the protective "dose." Measurable via parent-reported routine logs and, in longer deployments, validated sleep questionnaires (e.g., CSHQ — Children's Sleep Habits Questionnaire).

- **Impact B2 — Strengthened parent–child bond through accumulated positive shared experiences (inverts: "Weakened parent-child bond due to accumulated negative remote interactions").** By replacing bored silences and failed calls with playful, child-directed storytelling sessions, the app conditions the child to associate the remote parent with enjoyable activity. Measurable through longitudinal parent and child relationship quality assessments and through qualitative interview data on the child's eagerness to participate over time.

- **Impact B3 — Preserved shared reading habit as a vehicle for literacy development (inverts: "Loss of the shared reading habit eliminates a key vehicle for literacy development").** Raffle et al. (2010), citing the Mol et al. (2008) meta-analysis, positioned dialogic reading as a research-validated driver of language development. The app's PEER/CROWD prompts (Strategy A2) actively practise dialogic reading techniques during every session. Measurable by tracking the frequency and type of CROWD prompts completed per session and, in extended studies, by pre/post vocabulary assessments.

- **Impact B4 — Children develop positive attitudes toward remote communication rather than avoidance behaviours (inverts: "Children develop avoidance behaviours toward remote communication").** By making sessions intrinsically appealing (Strategy C4) and child-directed, the app aims to reverse the pattern identified by Yarosh & Abowd (2011) where children resisted parent-initiated contact. Observable through longitudinal tracking of child-initiated session requests (does the child ask for story time?) and through qualitative parent reports on the child's willingness to participate.

- **Impact B5 — The remote parent maintains an active co-parenting role during separation (inverts: "The remote parent's role shifts from active co-parent to passive observer").** The app positions the traveling parent as an active participant — reading aloud, controlling prompts, modelling gestures, and responding to the child's answers — rather than passively "checking in." The activity log and shared reading history (Strategy C3) provide tangible evidence of ongoing involvement. Measurable through parent self-report on perceived parenting role during separation and through the at-home caregiver's assessment of the remote parent's continued involvement.
