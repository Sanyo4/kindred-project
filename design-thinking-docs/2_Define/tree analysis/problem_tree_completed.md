# Problem Tree Analysis

## 1. Core Problem (The Trunk)

**Separated families lack a developmentally appropriate, activity-based digital tool to sustain consistent bedtime routines with preschool-aged children (3–7), resulting in degraded parent-child connection and disrupted sleep-related rituals during periods of work-related absence.**

---

## 2. Root Causes (The Roots)

### A. Developmental / Cognitive Factors (The Child)

- **Preschoolers lack the conversational skills required by existing communication tools.** Raffle et al. (2010) found that phone and video calls demand "sophisticated conversational skills (and young children rarely do)" possess them — meaning standard tools are structurally incompatible with the developmental stage of 2–4-year-olds.

- **Comprehension, not motor skill, is the primary barrier to touchscreen interaction.** Hiniker et al. (2015) demonstrated that when children understood a gesture, they executed it successfully 87% of the time. The bottleneck is cognitive — children cannot decode what a prompt is asking them to do, not that they cannot physically perform the action.

- **Visual-only prompts are ineffective for preschoolers and show no improvement with age.** Hiniker et al. (2015) found that visual state changes (pulsing, glowing, animating) were the least effective prompt type despite appearing in 41% of children's apps. They draw attention but cannot communicate what gesture to perform.

- **Children under 3 cannot respond to any in-app prompt without an adult co-user.** Hiniker et al. (2015) showed that audio and hand prompts were strongly age-dependent, with sharp performance gains only between ages 3 and 3.5. For the youngest users, adult modeling is the only effective instructional method.

- **Young children need shared activity, not conversation, to sustain engagement.** Raffle et al. (2010) observed that "even when co-located adults interact with young children they do not have a conversation, but rather they play together." Without a joint activity to anchor interaction, remote sessions with toddlers collapse within minutes.

### B. Technological Factors (The Current Tools)

- **Standard video chat provides no shared object of attention or joint context.** Raffle et al. (2010) framed this as a problem of "shared context and joint attention" — video calls offer faces but no common focus, leaving both parties with nothing concrete to do together.

- **Current app interfaces ignore preschooler cognitive and motor limitations.** Hiniker et al. (2015) found that 19% of children's apps used text-based prompts (useless for pre-literates), and only 3% used character demonstrations — the most effective approach after adult modeling.

- **Existing tools offer no mechanism for synchronising a shared reading experience.** Raffle et al. (2010) had to build a custom sensor-enhanced book frame with page-turn detection and a remote page icon to achieve synchronisation — functionality that no consumer tool provides natively.

- **Video communication tools were designed for adult conversation patterns.** Yarosh & Abowd (2011) studied families using Skype on laptops (the dominant tool in 2011), which required fixed locations, significant setup time, and sustained verbal exchange — none of which suit a preschooler's bedtime context.

- **No existing tool combines synchronous and asynchronous modes for family routines.** Yarosh & Abowd (2011) identified that families naturally combine "scheduled synchronous" and "spontaneous asynchronous" strategies, yet no single platform supports both within the same activity (e.g., live reading with a fallback to pre-recorded story segments).

### C. Contextual / Environmental Factors (The Situation)

- **Work-related separation is recurring, predictable, and pervasive.** Yarosh & Abowd (2011) cite broader family-separation prevalence figures and argue that work separation creates a distinct design space. It differs from divorce or deployment in being shorter, recurring, and often predictable.

- **Time zone differences make synchronous bedtime sessions impossible on many nights.** Yarosh & Abowd (2011) found that when schedules do not align, families lack effective fallback mechanisms — the bedtime routine simply does not happen.

- **The collocated (at-home) adult bears a heavy facilitation burden.** Yarosh & Abowd (2011) found that both the remote parent and the child "rely heavily on a collocated adult to maintain awareness and contact." For preschoolers who lack the literacy and technical skills to initiate communication, the at-home caregiver must set up devices, manage calls, and mediate the interaction.

- **Parent and child motivations for contact are asymmetric.** Yarosh & Abowd (2011) found that parents want "constant presence," while children prefer to live their lives and reconnect at reunion. Parents "initiate contact more often than their children," and children can find forced engagement "boring" or "intrusive."

- **Bedtime is a uniquely high-stakes routine window.** Mindell et al. (2015) established a dose-dependent positive association between consistent bedtime routines and children going to sleep. Disrupting this specific routine has measurable consequences, making it a higher-priority target than general-purpose communication.

## 3. Potential Consequences (The Branches)

### A. Immediate Effects (Short-term)

- **Children become bored, distracted, or refuse to engage during remote calls.** Yarosh & Abowd (2011) reported that children were "reluctant to engage in virtual contact with family members, finding it boring or they would rather be doing something else." Without a structured activity, attention drops within minutes.

- **Remote reading sessions collapse rapidly without scaffolding.** Raffle et al. (2010) found that standard video calls with young children lasted only a few minutes, compared to 15–20 minutes when anchored to a shared reading activity (StoryVisit follow-up data). Without an activity, there is nothing for the child to do.

- **Children experience frustration when they cannot understand app interactions.** Hiniker et al. (2015) demonstrated that visual-only prompts fail to communicate what action is required, leaving children confused and unable to participate. This frustration compounds when no adult is present to model the correct gesture.

- **The at-home caregiver experiences increased stress from the facilitation burden.** Yarosh & Abowd (2011) established that the collocated adult must set up technology, coax the child into participating, and manage the interaction — adding effort to an already demanding bedtime period, particularly when the setup is technically complex (as in Raffle et al.'s sensor-enhanced book frame).

- **Traveling parents experience guilt and a sense of exclusion from the child's routine.** Yarosh & Abowd (2011) found that parents sought "constant presence" but lacked the tools to achieve it. Failed or awkward calls reinforce the feeling of being absent from the child's daily life.

### B. Long-term Effects (Relationship / Developmental)

- **Degraded bedtime routine consistency leads to poorer sleep outcomes.** Mindell et al. (2015) demonstrated a dose-dependent relationship — fewer consistent bedtime routine nights are directly associated with worse sleep outcomes. Disrupted routines during separation periods reduce the "dose" and weaken the protective effect.

- **Weakened parent-child bond due to accumulated negative remote interactions.** Repeated experiences of bored silences, failed technology, and forced calls risk conditioning the child to associate the remote parent with unpleasant interactions rather than warmth. Yarosh & Abowd (2011) noted children could perceive contact attempts as "intrusive."

- **Loss of the shared reading habit eliminates a key vehicle for literacy development.** Raffle et al. (2010), drawing on Zevenbergen & Whitehurst (2003) and the Mol et al. (2008) meta-analysis, positioned dialogic reading as a research-validated driver of language development. When separation eliminates shared reading, the child loses both the relational and the educational benefits.

- **Children develop avoidance behaviours toward remote communication.** The asymmetry identified by Yarosh & Abowd (2011) — where parents push for contact and children resist — can escalate over time. If remote interactions are consistently boring or frustrating, children may refuse to participate at all, severing the primary connection channel.

- **The remote parent's role shifts from active co-parent to passive observer.** Yarosh & Abowd (2011) found that without tools supporting shared activities, the traveling parent is reduced to "checking in" rather than participating in the child's routine. Over time, this diminishes the parent's perceived involvement and authority within the family dynamic.
